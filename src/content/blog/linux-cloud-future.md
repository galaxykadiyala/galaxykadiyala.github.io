IBM Canada made an interesting argument: Linux is the cloud's future.

It's a position that's hard to argue with. Cloud computing at its core is about shared infrastructure, standardization, and economics of scale — all things that open source and Linux are uniquely positioned to deliver.

Proprietary operating systems come with licensing costs that multiply with every virtual machine. Linux doesn't. When you're running thousands of instances, that difference is enormous.

## Why It Makes Sense

The cloud is fundamentally about abstraction — removing the hardware layer from the equation, letting software define the infrastructure. Linux, with its modularity, stability, and open development model, is perfectly suited to that environment.

The major cloud providers already knew this. Amazon EC2 ran on Linux. Google's infrastructure ran on Linux. IBM was just stating the obvious — but it mattered that a company of that size was saying it publicly.

## The Open Web Connection

For those of us in the Mozilla world, this wasn't just an infrastructure story. It was further proof that open source wasn't a hobby or an ideology — it was becoming the foundation of the entire technology industry.

Open source wins because the economics make sense and the collaboration model produces better software. The cloud was just the latest proof point.
