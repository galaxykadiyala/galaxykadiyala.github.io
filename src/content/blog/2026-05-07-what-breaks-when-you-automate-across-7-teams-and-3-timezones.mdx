Eight months. Seven teams. Three timezones. One TPM.

Two TPMs before me had been replaced inside two months because the program was off the rails. By the time I joined, the client was running out of patience and the teams were demoralised. The brief was simple: stabilise the program, deliver the work, repair the relationship. No pressure.

We delivered in ten months, not eight. The client publicly thanked the team and singled out my work. Here's what those ten months actually looked like.

## What we were building

The program: automate test coverage across an enterprise client's full product suite, UI and backend, deliver across teams in India, Europe, and the US, and hand off a working, pipeline-connected automation framework at the end.

The client had seven product teams, each owning a different product in their portfolio. Some were pure backend, some had significant UI surfaces, and a few had both. The stack was mixed: Selenium and Python on the UI side, Golang on the backend. No shared test framework, no shared pipeline, and critically, no prior manual QA baseline. Tests weren't written before something broke. They were written after.

My job as TPM was to coordinate end-to-end: requirements gathering across all seven teams, backlog creation, sprint planning, cross-timezone handoffs, dependency sequencing, and delivery tracking. I wasn't writing tests. I was making sure the right tests got written in the right order by the right teams without anyone waiting on anyone else longer than necessary.

## Month one: the structure we built

The first thing I did was run discovery calls with each team. Not a single kickoff, individual calls, because the products were different enough that a group session would've wasted five teams' time while one team answered questions.

From those calls we chartered stories and tasks for each team, built out the backlog, and established a formal handoff model between timezones. India handed to Europe, Europe handed to US, US handed back to India. Each handoff had a defined artifact: what was done, what was blocked, what needed a decision. Nobody waited 12 hours for an answer that could travel with the work.

For the first month, this worked. Teams were moving, backlogs were burning down, pipelines were being set up. The program felt under control.

## What we planned for before we started

One thing I knew going in: an 8-month program across 7 teams with mixed seniority levels and high delivery pressure would take a toll on people. Attrition, performance issues, burnout — these weren't risks to monitor passively. They were near-certainties to plan for actively.

So during the initial planning phase, before a single sprint started, we built a full onboarding kit for the program. Written docs covering each product, its architecture, its test surface, and its dependencies. Video walkthroughs so someone joining mid-program could self-serve without pulling a senior engineer away for two weeks of handholding. The goal was a two-week ramp: any new person joining at any point could be productive within a fortnight.

This felt like overhead at kickoff. It paid for itself twice over.

Midway through the program, a few engineers were not performing at the level the timeline required. Not effort issues, output issues. The work wasn't meeting the bar and the program couldn't absorb the drag. I put them on PIPs, documented the gaps clearly, gave them runway to course-correct, and when it was clear they wouldn't, made the call to replace them.

Replacing engineers mid-program on a cross-timezone delivery is exactly the kind of thing that derails schedules. Except we'd planned for it. The onboarding docs and videos meant replacements were up to speed in two weeks, not six. The KT process was already defined. The ramp was already designed.

The proactive planning didn't prevent the people problem. It just meant the people problem didn't become a program problem.

## Month two: everything broke

The second month is where the program fell apart.

Tests were failing across multiple teams simultaneously. Not one team's tests, several teams' tests, in ways that didn't map cleanly to the code those teams had written. A backend service test would fail because a dependency it relied on had changed. A UI test would fail because an API it called had been modified by a different team. The failures were cascading, and the root cause wasn't code quality. It was sequencing.

We had no dependency map.

I had charted stories and tasks per team. I had not charted the relationships between those teams' work. Which service depended on which other service. Which API contract needed to be stable before another team could test against it. Which backend changes would break existing UI automation. None of that was visible.

This was my mistake. Not the teams', mine. Dependency mapping is a TPM responsibility and I had skipped it.

## The fix: building the map we should've had on day one

We paused active development for a week and did nothing but map dependencies. Every team, every service, every API contract, every shared data path. By the end of the week we had a graph that showed us exactly why things were breaking: three services sat at the centre of most of the dependency chains, and we'd been letting different teams modify them in parallel without coordination.

With the map in hand, we re-sequenced the work. Core services were locked down first, contracts frozen, tests written against stable versions. Product teams could then build their automation against a known-good foundation instead of a moving target.

The cascading failures stopped within two weeks of implementing the new sequencing.

## The pipeline problem

Partway through the program we hit a second issue: the existing CI/CD pipelines didn't support what we were building. They'd been designed for the old manual QA process, not automated test suites running across multiple services with cross-team dependencies.

Rather than patch them, we made a deliberate call: hire a dedicated DevOps engineer to rebuild them properly. That hire added time to the program, but it was the right trade. Patching a pipeline designed for a different workflow would've created fragility that we'd pay for every sprint for the rest of the program.

The new pipelines handled both testing and deployment, ran the right suites against the right services in the right order, and gave teams visibility into what was passing and failing without needing to ask anyone.

## What the two extra months were actually about

The 8-to-10-month overrun had two causes, and they're worth separating because they're different problems with different solutions.

**The dependency crisis** added roughly three to four weeks. One week to build the map, two to three weeks to re-sequence and stabilise. This was recoverable because we caught it before it cascaded further. If we'd hit month four or five before finding it, the overrun would have been worse.

**Late-arriving requirements** added the rest. Several teams didn't have complete test specifications at kickoff because their products hadn't been manually QA'd before. Test cases were being written or changed reactively, when something failed rather than before automation began. This is a scope definition problem, and it's endemic to programs where the client hasn't done the foundational work before asking for automation on top of it.

The lesson there: **automation programs need a manual QA baseline first.** You can not automate a process that hasn't been understood manually. If the test cases don't exist, the automation timeline is a fiction.

## What I'd do differently

**Build the onboarding kit during planning, not when you need it.** Onboarding docs and video walkthroughs feel like overhead in week one. They feel like lifesavers in month five when you're replacing someone mid-program. If the program is long enough that people might leave, it's long enough to need a self-serve ramp. Build it before the first sprint, not in response to the first departure.

**Build the dependency map before writing a single task.** Not during discovery, not in month two. Before the backlog exists. The map is the foundation everything else is sequenced on. Without it, you're coordinating effort without coordinating dependencies, which is not coordination at all.

**Require a manual QA sign-off as a program entry criterion.** If a product team can't show you a documented manual test suite before automation starts, their automation stream isn't ready to start. Scope it out until they can. Accepting undefined scope in exchange for a cleaner kickoff is a trade that always costs you later.

**Audit the pipelines before month one.** CI/CD pipeline health is infrastructure, and infrastructure problems compound. A broken pipeline on day thirty is a broken pipeline that's been silently shaping decisions for thirty days. Check it early, fix it or replace it before the teams depend on it.

**Formalise the handoff artifact from day one.** This one we got right, and it's why the timezone coordination never became a crisis. Every handoff had three fields: done, blocked, needs decision. Teams in Europe didn't have to reconstruct context from Slack. Teams in the US didn't wait until the India morning standup to find out something was blocked. The artifact travelled with the work.

## How I actually run programs

Something a colleague said about my work on this program stuck with me, and it's roughly how I think about the role:

> "You may not be seen in threads, emails, or messages. But you get the job done."

That's the version of TPM work I believe in. The job isn't to be visible in every channel. The job is to make sure the work happens, the dependencies are tracked, the people are unblocked, and the program ships. If I'm in every Slack thread, I'm probably not doing the actual job, I'm performing the job.

This program was a test of that philosophy. Two TPMs had been visible and active and the program was still failing. What it needed wasn't more activity. It needed someone to map the work properly, sequence it correctly, replace the people who couldn't keep up, plan for the things that would break, and then let the teams do their jobs.

In the end, all that matters is getting the job done.

## Postscript

This program ran during my time at Aziro Technologies as a TPM coordinating a client engagement. Seven teams, three timezones, mixed stack, no prior manual QA baseline. We delivered. The dependency map mistake was the most instructive thing that happened in ten months, because it made visible something that's easy to skip: coordinating people is not the same as coordinating the work. The work has its own dependencies, and those need their own map.
